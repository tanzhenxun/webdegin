Japan Festival

Japanese festivals are traditional festive occasions often celebrated with dance and music in Japan. Many festivals have their roots in traditional Chinese festivals, but have undergone extensive changes over time to have little resemblance to their original form, despite sharing the same name and date. There are also various local festivals (e.g. Tobata Gion) that are mostly unknown outside a given prefecture.

Japanese New Year

The Japanese New Year is the most important festival of the year for the Yamato people in Japan and around the world, and is also known as the first month of the year (Shōgatsu, Shōgache). It was originally the first month of the lunar calendar, but after the Meiji Restoration, the Grecian calendar was adopted and used to refer to the new calendar month of January, which is known as the first month until 31 January.

The history of the Japanese New Year is generally believed to date back to the Hitori period, when Emperor Taiko introduced the then lunar calendar, the Genkai calendar, from the Tang dynasty in China and followed the customs of the Tang people, including the custom of celebrating the New Year, and since then the Japanese have been celebrating the New Year in the first month of the lunar calendar.

Call the god of the fields (the god of the grain harvest), who has returned to the mountains, to return to the mortal world; it is not advisable to cook on fire when the gods are present.

1 January to 3 January in the Gregorian calendar

Activities

House Cleaning

House Cleaning

The Japanese traditionally start cleaning their shrines on December 13, both to clean their homes and to wash away bad luck.

New Year's Eve Bell

New Year's Eve Bell

At midnight on New Year's Eve, 108 bells are rung at Buddhist temples of all sizes throughout the country, known as the New Year's Eve bell ringing.

Year-end-party

Year-end-party

Year-end-party is a company or friends' dinner, a custom from the Muromachi period. It means a party to forget the hard work of the year.

New Year's Shopping

New Year's Shopping

In late December, there is a New Year's Eve market called "Toshinomichi" (New Year's Eve market) all over Japan, selling various New Year's Eve items.

Gion Matsuri

The Gion Festival, also known as the Gion Goryo-kai, is an annual festival held in July in the Higashiyama district of Kyoto, Japan. The entire Gion Festival lasts for a month, with a large parade taking place on 17 July (the former festival) and 24 July (the latter festival).

In 862 AD, a series of disasters combined with wet weather led to a massive outbreak of epidemics. The plague was successfully quelled after the Gion Festival in Higashiyama, Kyoto, dedicated the Bull-headed Heavenly King as a deity.

In Kyoto, a meeting of the spirits of the spirits of the plague was held.

17 July and 24 July

Activities

Shinko Matsuri

Shinko Matsuri

Shinkosai is one of the Shinto rituals, meaning 'to pray for the presence of the deity'. It is a ritual held when the deities depart from the head office.

Hanagasa Parade

Hanagasa Parade

The Hanagasa parade is a procession of Hanagasa as the centrepiece of Gion drums, caravan samurai, child samurai, heron, miso-grilled skewered tofu, and manjan jumping from Yasaka Shrine to Teradori and Shijo Gyojusho.

Lantern Festival

Lantern Festival

Members carry various lanterns in a parade around Hachiban Shrine to welcome the mikoshi (portable shrine) for the upcoming mikoshi ceremony.

Yamahoko Parade

Yamahoko Parade

The Yamahoko Parade, as the name suggests, is a parade of surimi (surimi) that goes around and around. There are now 33 surahoko (mountain surahoko) in the Gion festival, of which 23 are in the former festival and 10 in the latter.
